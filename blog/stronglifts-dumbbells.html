DUMBLIFTS

StrongLifts 5×5 With Dumbbells: The No-Barbell Alternative

Same proven method. Different equipment. Real results.

The Problem

StrongLifts 5×5 is one of the most popular beginner strength programs ever created. Squat, bench, row, overhead press, deadlift — five sets of five, three days a week, add weight every session. It works. The problem is it requires a barbell, a squat rack, and a bench.

A lot of us don't have that. Maybe you train at a gym that only has dumbbells. Maybe you work out at home and a full barbell setup isn't in the budget or the space. Maybe you're traveling and the hotel gym has a dumbbell rack and nothing else.

You don't need to skip the program. You need to adapt it.

The Dumbbell Substitutions

Every barbell lift in StrongLifts has a dumbbell equivalent that works the same muscle groups through the same movement pattern:

Barbell → Dumbbell

Barbell Squat Goblet Squat
Barbell Bench Press Bench Press
Barbell Row Row
Barbell Overhead Press Overhead Press
Barbell Deadlift Romanian Deadlift

The movements are the same. The loading is different — dumbbells force each side of your body to work independently, which actually builds better stability and fixes strength imbalances that barbells can hide.

The A/B Split

Just like StrongLifts, you alternate two workouts. Three days a week. Rest days in between.

  • Week 1: A / B / A
  • Week 2: B / A / B
  • Keep alternating.

Each workout has one legs exercise, one push, and one pull. Your whole body gets trained every session.

Progression Is Different

This is the one real difference. With a barbell, you add 5 pounds total per session. With dumbbells, the smallest jump is usually 5 pounds per hand — so 10 pounds total. That's a bigger leap.

The fix: don't rush the jumps. Stay at your current weight until it feels easy. Push for extra reps — 5×6 or 5×7 — before moving up. When you do jump, expect to drop back to 5×3 or 5×4. Build back to 5×5 over a few sessions. This is the normal dumbbell progression cycle.

What You Lose (And What You Gain)

You lose: The ability to microload. Barbell programs can add weight almost every session. Dumbbell progression is slower and happens in bigger steps.

You gain: Unilateral strength (each arm works independently), better stability, more joint-friendly pressing, and the ability to train anywhere with minimal equipment. You also don't need a spotter — failed dumbbell reps are easy to bail from safely.
