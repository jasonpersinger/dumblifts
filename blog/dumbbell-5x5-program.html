The Dumbbell 5×5 Program: Build Strength With What You've Got

No barbell required. Just dumbbells, consistency, and progressive overload.

What Is 5×5?

5×5 is one of the oldest and most proven strength training methods. You do five sets of five reps on compound exercises, lifts that work multiple muscle groups at once. It's simple, it's effective, and it doesn't require living in the gym.

The original programs like StrongLifts and Starting Strength are built around barbells. But not everyone has access to a barbell. Maybe you train at a YMCA, a hotel gym, or at home with a set of dumbbells. That's where the dumbbell 5×5 comes in.

How It Works

You alternate between two workouts, three days per week. One day on, one day off. The classic split looks like this:

Workout A

Goblet Squat 5×5

Bench Press 5×5

Row 5×5

Workout B

Romanian Deadlift 5×5

Overhead Press 5×5

Floor Press 5×5

Week one you go A/B/A. Week two you go B/A/B. Keep alternating. That's it.

Why It Works

Every workout hits your full body through push, pull, and legs movement patterns. The volume is moderate enough to recover from in 48 hours, but heavy enough to drive real strength gains. The key is progressive overload, increasing the weight over time as you get stronger.

Progressive Overload With Dumbbells

With a barbell, you can add 2.5 or 5 pounds per session. Dumbbells typically jump in 5-pound increments per hand, which is a bigger leap. Here's how to handle it:

  1. Stay at your current weight until 5×5 feels solid, even comfortable.
  2. Push for extra reps (5×6 or 5×7) before jumping up.
  3. Make the jump. You might only get 5×3 at the new weight. That's fine.
  4. Build back to 5×5 at the new weight over a few sessions.

If you fail to hit 5×5 at a weight for three sessions in a row, deload 10% and build back up. This is normal and part of the process.

Who Is This For?

Anyone who wants to get stronger with dumbbells. Beginners who need a program that actually works. Experienced lifters who need to train with limited equipment. People who don't want to overthink their training.

You don't need a fancy gym. You don't need a coach. You need dumbbells and a plan.

Rest Between Sets

90 seconds when the weight is light. 3 to 5 minutes when it gets heavy. Don't rush it. The point is to lift heavy, and that requires recovery between sets.
